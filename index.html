Academy Overview

The first civil academy for teaching tent pegging in the Arab Republic of Egypt.

  • The first academy accredited by the Egyptian Equestrian Federation.
  • Under the supervision and training of Captain\ Aya Issa, the only accredited international trainer and referee, Bishara Dhahab.
  • The first academy in Egypt that relies on studying horse behavior, teaching horse riding, principles of sports psychology and field injuries.
  • Established in December 2022, on an area of ​​12,000 meters with full services.

Aya Issa's Profile

Aya Issa, a trailblazer in the sport of tent pegging

She is the first and only certified international coach of the sport in Egypt, and she is also a highly esteemed figure both nationally and internationally. Her significant contributions as a coach, competitor, and international judge have played a pivotal role in shaping the future of tent pegging in Egypt. Currently, she serves as the coach of the Armed Forces team and holds the prestigious distinction of a gold badge as an international referee.

Familiarize Yourself

Horses have unique traits and behaviors that even equestrians may not know. From recognizing human emotions to their unusual sleeping habits, these animals exhibit fascinating characteristics. Slide to know more.

Smile
Horses express pleasure through their upper lip, a facial expression called the "Flehmen response," which involves curling their lips and inhaling when rubbed, scratched, or groomed.
Stand-up Sleep
Horses do lay down for deeper sleep, with an average of 2.5 hours of sleep per day, contrary to the myth that they never lay down.
Vision
Horses have a 360-degree field of vision, but have two blind spots behind and in front of their head, relying on their mobile senses and lips.
Hearing
Horses have 10 muscles for ear control, allowing them to rotate their ears nearly 180 degrees and move them independently.
Lifespan
Horses can live up to 30 years with a high quality of life, but without proper care, they may only live below 20 years.
Snorts
A horse's snort, along with other positive visual indicators, indicates happiness, peace, and comfort, making them likely to feel calm and comfortable.
Ticklishness
Horses are sensitive to specific spots on their bodies, especially their bellies or jaws, and can react with twitching, movement, or playful nipping.
Head Hooves – Horses need their hooves cleaned daily to remove dirt, stones, and debris. Regular trimming and shoeing are also essential to maintain hoof health.
Body Coat – Grooming the coat helps remove dirt, sweat, and loose hair, and can prevent skin irritation. Regular brushing stimulates blood circulation and promotes healthy fur.
Leg Teeth – Horse teeth should be checked regularly, typically once a year, by a veterinarian. They can develop sharp points that need filing to prevent discomfort while eating.
Eyes Eyes – Keep an eye on the horse's eyes for any signs of infection, irritation, or injury. Clean around the eyes to avoid buildup.
Mane Mane and Tail – These should be brushed and untangled frequently to prevent matting. The tail should also be cleaned to prevent infections in the tailbone area.
Nostrils Nostrils and Ears – Check the nostrils and ears for signs of discharge or infection. Gently clean the inside of the ears if needed.
Joints Legs and Joints – Check the legs for signs of swelling, cuts, or bruises, especially after exercise or trail riding.
